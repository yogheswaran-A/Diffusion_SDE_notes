Geometric Brownian Motion & Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Process

Geometric Brownian Motion

Brownian motion can have both positive and negative values as long as its mean is centered around zero and the distribution over time follows the characteristics of the Wiener process. Yet certain quantities can only have positive values such as stocks.

In order to accomodate the specific requires of such quantities we can work with geometric Brownian motion, dStdStSt=μtStdt+σtStdWt=μtdt+σtdWt(1)(2)(1)dSt=μtStdt+σtStdWt(2)dStSt=μtdt+σtdWt which has the nice property that as StSt approaches zero, so does the change. This effectively limits StSt to positive values, St≥0St≥0.

Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Process

The Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) process is a SDE that exhibits mean reversion and momentum properties. Mathematically it is defined as: dXt=θ(μ−Xt)dt+σdWt(15)(15)dXt=θ(μ−Xt)dt+σdWt where θθ is the momentum parameter that makes the OU process undulate around the mean. The mean parameter μμ sets the value around which the OU process moves in somewhat smooth arcs. Visually it looks like this:

where we can see that even though two of the three sample paths start far away from the mean, they quickly converge to a region around the mean. Once in the vicinity of the mean they move about it in arcs through the momentum factor.

The first time I heard of the OU process was in a reinforcement learning paper where it was used to force an agent to repeat the same action a couple of times through the momentum property.

First, let’s try to clean up the notation to something more succinct and define a new random variable YtYt: Yt=Xt−μ(16)(16)Yt=Xt−μ We can easily compute the infinitesimal differential dYtdYt of YtYt via: dYt=dXt=θ(μ−Xt)dt+σdWt=−θ(Xt−μ)Ytdt+σdWt=−θYtdt+σdWt(17)(18)(19)(20)(17)dYt=dXt(18)=θ(μ−Xt)dt+σdWt(19)=−θ(Xt−μ)⏟Ytdt+σdWt(20)=−θYtdt+σdWt The next step is to recognize that we are equating the derivative of a random variable with itself. We’ll abuse the mathematical notation for brief period to make the point more clear: dYtdYtdt∝θYtdt∝θYt⇔deaxdx=aex(21)(22)(21)dYt∝θYtdt(22)dYtdt∝θYt⇔deaxdx=aex The proportional equation above has a striking similarity to the derivative of a scaled exponential eaxeax. In our case the derivative is not with respect to xx but to tt. But since ZtZt is a function of a stochastic process we will have to apply Ito’s lemma in order to compute the differential. Since Zt=f(t,θ,Yt)Zt=f(t,θ,Yt) is linear in YtYt, we will deal with a simplified version of Ito’s lemma because the second derivative of a linear function is zero: df(t,θ,Yt)=∂t f(t,θ,Yt)dt+∂Yt f(t,θ,Yt)dYt+12∂2Yt f(t,θ,Yt)=0dY2t=∂t f(t,θ,Yt)dt+∂Yt f(t,θ,Yt)dYt(25)(26)(25)df(t,θ,Yt)=∂t f(t,θ,Yt)dt+∂Yt f(t,θ,Yt)dYt+12∂Yt2 f(t,θ,Yt)⏞=0dYt2(26)=∂t f(t,θ,Yt)dt+∂Yt f(t,θ,Yt)dYt Applying the simplified Ito’s lemma to our equation at hand yields: dZt=∂t[eθtYt]dt+∂Yt[eθtYt]dYt=θeθtYtdt+eθtdYt=θeθtYtdt+eθt(−θYtdt+σdWt)=eθtσdWt(27)(28)(29)(30)(27)dZt=∂t[eθtYt]dt+∂Yt[eθtYt]dYt(28)=θeθtYtdt+eθtdYt(29)=θeθtYtdt+eθt(−θYtdt+σdWt)(30)=eθtσdWt This can be easily solved via ZT=ZS+∫TSdZt=ZS+σ∫TSeθtdWt(31)(32)(31)ZT=ZS+∫STdZt(32)=ZS+σ∫STeθtdWt where SS is the start of the integration through time. If the momentum is only small, the Wiener process can exert a stronger influence and the stationary distribution has a wider variance.

More importantly, since the Wiener process is the only random influence on the process and is Gaussian, the entire process is a Gaussian process. Thus the stationary distribution is a Gaussian distribution as well.
